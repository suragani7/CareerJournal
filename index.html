Introduction

About Me

I am Sai Lahari Suragani, a cybersecurity graduate student at the University of Delaware. My interest in digital security began not in a classroom, but at home — through stories my father, a police officer, shared about people being cheated, scammed, and robbed in ways they never saw coming, all because of the internet.

Over the years, that curiosity grew into a career. I have worked with police investigators, security research firms, and built my own security tools from scratch. This diary tells that story — from the moment I first heard the word “cybercrime” to where I stand today.

Chronology

The Journey,
Year by Year

2020 — The Turning Point
Why Cybersecurity?
Home, India · During COVID-19 Lockdown

I had just finished my 12th board exams when the world went into lockdown. Everyone was stuck at home, and almost overnight, the internet became the only way people could work, shop, talk, and survive. But it also became a hunting ground.

My father, who worked as a police officer, started bringing home stories I could not ignore — people losing their life savings to scammers they had never met, fake websites draining bank accounts, families tricked through messages that looked completely real. These were not distant news headlines. These were real people in real trouble, and the crimes were happening faster than anyone knew how to stop them.

That was the moment I understood what cybersecurity actually meant. Not just firewalls and code — but protecting people. I decided then that this was the field I wanted to be part of.

2020 – 2024
Bachelor of Engineering in Computer Science
Geethanjali College of Engineering & Technology · Hyderabad, India · GPA 3.2/4.0

I joined Geethanjali College with a clear goal: learn everything I could about cybersecurity. The program had a dedicated specialization in the field, which covered everything from how to break into systems (ethically) to how encryption actually keeps data safe.

Courses like Cryptography, Digital Forensics, Ethical Hacking, Cloud Computing, Mobile Network Security, and Cyber Laws gave me a strong base. I also became Technical Head of the Cyber Warriors Club, where I organized seminars to teach students about online safety and cyber hygiene. Two of my technical poster presentations — one on cyberbullying, another on why security is non-negotiable today — each won “Best Poster” at their competitions.

I was also part of the National Service Scheme, volunteering in community drives like Swachh Bharat, health campaigns, and computer education for school children.

July – August 2021
Cybersecurity Intern
Coincent.ai

My first real hands-on experience with hacking tools. I learned to use Kali Linux to crack passwords and gather information, tested web applications for common weaknesses using Burp Suite and OWASP ZAP, and studied how malware behaves and how network traffic looks when an attack is happening.

The internship walked me through the full ethical hacking process — from reconnaissance all the way to exploitation — using a cloud-based AWS lab. I practiced on intentionally vulnerable apps like DVWA and OWASP Juice Shop, finding SQL injection flaws, XSS bugs, and authentication weaknesses. It was the first time I felt like a real security professional, not just a student.

June – July 2022
Cybersecurity Summer Intern
Gurugram Police — Cyber Crime Unit

This was the experience that connected everything for me. I worked alongside actual cybercrime investigators — the people who deal with phishing, online fraud, malware attacks, and financial scams every day. Seeing how these cases unfold in real life, and how investigators piece together what happened, made everything I had studied feel completely real.

I supported threat analysis, learned how digital forensics is done on actual evidence, and saw how indicators of compromise are identified in live investigations. This internship reminded me exactly why I chose this field: real people are affected, and the work you do genuinely matters.

February – November 2023
Junior Security Researcher
Ironwaspinfo Security Pvt. Ltd · THUB Incubation Hub, Hyderabad

My first professional role in a security firm. I focused on client-side web security — specifically Magecart attacks, where hackers secretly inject scripts into checkout pages to steal payment data. I also looked at cross-site scripting (XSS) vulnerabilities and risks from third-party scripts that companies embed on their websites without thinking twice.

Over eight months, I wrote more than 50 threat analysis reports, each explaining what was found, how serious it was, and what the client should do about it. I also helped build an internal Cybersecurity Dictionary so everyone on the team used the same language when describing threats. It was the kind of work where being precise and clear mattered as much as being technically right.

August 2024 – Present
Master of Science in Cybersecurity
University of Delaware · Newark, Delaware, USA · GPA 3.74/4.0

Graduate school at UD has pushed me further than I expected. The program mixes deep theory with very practical work — from penetration testing and reverse engineering to cloud security and digital forensics at a professional level.

Courses include Penetration Testing, Reverse Engineering, Ethical Hacking, Web Application Security, Secure Software Design, System Hardening, Cloud Computing & Security, and Digital Forensics. The pace is fast, the expectations are high, and I have genuinely learned something new every single week.

September – November 2024
Project: Network Security Analysis Tools
University of Delaware

I built three custom security tools from scratch using Python, Scapy, and Go. The first was an encrypted reverse proxy that tunnels traffic securely using AES-256. The second was an ARP poisoning detector — a tool that watches your local network and alerts you if someone is trying to intercept your traffic using a man-in-the-middle attack. The third was a TCP fingerprinting tool I called “synprobe,” which quietly identifies what operating systems and services are running on a network without touching them.

Building these from the ground up meant understanding not just how to use security tools, but how they actually work inside.

February – May 2025
Project: Ransomware Policy Design (NIST CSF 2.0)
University of Delaware

Healthcare organizations are among the most targeted by ransomware — and also among the least able to afford downtime. For this project, I designed a complete ransomware security policy for a healthcare organization, built around the updated NIST Cybersecurity Framework 2.0.

The policy covered all six pillars of the framework: Govern, Identify, Protect, Detect, Respond, and Recover. Every recommendation was written with the real-world constraints of a healthcare environment in mind — because a policy that is technically perfect but practically impossible to follow is useless.

January – March 2026
Project: Threat Objective Analysis
University of Delaware

My most recent project focused on analyzing a real organization’s threat landscape from the ground up. Starting from structured threat modeling, I mapped out every significant threat, scored each one by how likely it was and how much damage it could cause, and then produced a prioritized set of recommendations written for decision-makers — not just technical teams.

The goal was to translate complex threat data into clear, actionable business guidance. Security only works when the right people understand it and act on it.
